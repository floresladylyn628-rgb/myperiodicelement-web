Gadolinium is a silvery-white, ductile rare earth metal with atomic number 64, part of the lanthanide series. It is known for its magnetic properties, being ferromagnetic at room temperature and strongly paramagnetic above its Curie point.

Gadolinium was discovered in 1880 by Swiss chemist Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac, who identified it in the rare earth mineral gadolinite, which is named after Finnish chemist Johan Gadolin. Marignac used spectroscopic analysis to detect unique spectral lines, indicating the presence of a new element. In 1886, French chemist Paul-Emile Lecoq de Boisbaudran successfully isolated gadolinium in its oxide form.

Gadolinium has various applications, including:


Medical imaging: Gadolinium compounds are widely used to enhance images in Magnetic Resonance Imaging due to its paramagnetic properties.


Alloys: Incorporated into ferrous alloys to improve resistance to oxidation and high-temperature performance.


Data storage: Gadolinium is used in magneto-optical storage devices, taking advantage of its magnetic properties.
