Magnesium is a silvery-white, moderately hard, and relatively lightweight alkaline earth metal two valence electrons in its outermost energy level, making it relatively reactive. It's is highly flammable and burns with white light.

Magnesium was first isolated by Sir Humpry Davy in 1808 through electrolysis of a mixture of mercury oxide and magnesia (magnesium oxide). Davy named the element after the Greek region of Magnesia, where magnesite (magnesium carbonate) was found. Initially , magnesium was produced in small quantities, but industrial production began in the late 19th century. Today, magnesium is produced through various methods, including electrolysis and silicothermic reduction.

Magnesium has various applications, including:


Aerospace industry: Magnesium is used in aircraft and spacecraft components due to its lightweight and high strength-to-weight ratio.


Automotive industry: Magnesium is used in car parts, such as engine blocks, transmission cases, and steering wheels, to reduce weight and improve fuel efficiency.


Medical implants: Magnesium is used in some medical implants, such as bone implants and stents, due to its biocompatibility and biodegradability.
