Bohrium is a synthetic, highly radioactive element. It is not found in nature, exixts only in tiny quantities in laboratories, and has no known practical applications due to its extreme instability and short half-life. Bohrium is believed to be a metal and is chemically similar to rhenium, located below it in group 7 of the periodic table.

Bohrium is discovered by a German team at the GSI Center for Heavy Ion Research in Darmstadt in 1981, following earlier claims of synthesis by a Soviet team in 1976. It is named after the Nobel Prize-winning Danish physicist Niels Bohr, and was officially named and approved by the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry(IUPAC) in 1997. Bohrium is a transuranic, radioactive element produced in particle accelerators and is currently only used for scientific research.

Bohrium has no practical applications becuase of its extreme instability, rarity, and radioactive nature, which make it unsuitable for commercial, industrial, or medical use. Its solely utility lies in fundamental scientific research, where it serves as a subject of study to understand the behavior of superheavy elements, nuclear physics, atomic structures, and nuclear theories.
