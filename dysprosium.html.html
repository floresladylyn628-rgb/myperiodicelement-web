Dysprosium is a silvery-gray, soft, and highly reactive rare earth metal with atomic number 66, part of the lanthanide series. It is known for its magnetic strength and thermal stability.

Dysprosium was discovered in 1886 by French chemist Paul-Emile Lecoq de Boisbaudran while studying the rare earth mineral holmia. Using spectroscopic analysis, he identified new spectral lines that revealed the presence of a previously known element. Dysprosium's name is derived from the Greek word "dysprositos", meaning "hard to obtain", due to the challenges in isolating it from other rare earth elements.

Dysprosium has various applications, including:


Nuclear reactors: Dysprosium is an excellent neutron absorber, used in control rods and sheilding materials in nuclear reactors.


Lighting: Dysprosium compounds are used in halide lamps, producing bright white light for industrial and theatrical lighting.


Laser materials: Dysprosium-doped materials are utilized in infrared lasers for scientific and military applications.
