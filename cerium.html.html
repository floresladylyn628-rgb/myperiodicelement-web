Cerium is a silvery-gray, soft, and ductile metal, belonging to the lanthanide series of rare earth elements. It is the most abundant rare earth element, found in minerals such as monazite and bastnasite. Cerium is highly reactive, tarnishes quickly in air, and reacts with water to produce hydrogen gas.

Cerium was discovered in 1803 independently by two chemists: Jons Jakob Berzelius and Wilhelm Hisinger in Sweden, and Martin Heinrich Klaproth in Germany. The element was named after the asteroid "Ceres", which had been discovered two years earlier in 1801. Berzelius and Hisinger identified cerium while analyzing the mineral cerite, whereas Klaproth confirmed the discovery through his own studies.

Cerium has various applications, including:


Electronics: Cerium is used in phosphors for color television screens, flourescent lamps, and LEDs.


Self-cleaning ovens: Cerium oxide acts as a catalyst in self-cleaning oven coatings, helping break down food residues at high temperatures.


Glass polishing: Cerium is a key component in polishing powders used to polish glass, mirrors, and optical lenses.
