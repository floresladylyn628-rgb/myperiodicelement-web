Iridium is a silvery-white, dense, and corrosion-resistant metal, known as one of the densest elements and for its exceptional resistance to oxidation and chemical attack, even at high temperatures.

Iridium was discovered in 1803 by English chemist Smithson Tennant wile studying the insoluble residue left after dissolving platinum ore in aqua regia. Along with osmium, iridium was identified as a new element in this residue. Tennant named it iridium after the Latin word "iris," meaning "rainbow," because of the colorful salts it forms.

Iridium has various applications, including:


Radiotherapy: Iridium-192, a radioactive isotope, is used in cancer treatment via brachytherapy.


Satellite and spacecraft components: Used in parts that must withstand extreme conditions in space.


Crucibles: Used for growing single crystals and handling molten materials at extremely high temperatures.
