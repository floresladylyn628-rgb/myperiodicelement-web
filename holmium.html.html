Holmium is a silvery-white, soft, and malleable rare earth metal with atomic number 67, belonging to the lanthanide series. Holmium is notable for its exceptionally high magnetic strength.

Holmium was discovered in 1878 by Swiss chemists Marc Delafontaine and Jacques-Louis Soret, who identified it spectroscopically while studying rare earth elements. They notice unique spectral lines indicating a new element, which they initially referred to as "Element X." Later that year, Swedish chemist Per Teodor Cleve independently confirmed its existence and successfully separated it from the mineral erbium oxide. Cleve named the element holmium, after Holmia, the Latin word name for Stockholm, Sweden.

Holmium has various applications, including:


Glass coloring: Holmium compounds are used to create yellow or red glass and as calibration standards for spectrophotometers.


Nuclear reactors: Holmium is used as a neutron absorber in nuclear reactors, helping to control the fission process.


Alloys: Holmium is added to magnetic alloys to improve their performance in high-strength magnets and other magnetic applications.
