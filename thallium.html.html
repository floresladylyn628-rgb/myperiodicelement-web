Thallium is a soft, silvery-gray metal. It belongs to group 13 of the periodic table and is highly malleable but also toxic. Found in trace amounts in ores of other metals, thallium was discovered in 1861 by William Crookes through spectroscopy.

It was in 1861 when Crookes discovered thallium while analyzing residues from sulfuric acid production using flame spectroscopy. He observed a bright green spectral line, which led him to name the element thallium(from the Greek word "thallos", meaning "green shot" or "twig") because of the green color in its spectral signature.

Thallium has various applications, including:


Semiconductors: Thallium is used in certain semiconducting materials for electronics.


Photocells: Thallium sulfide and thallium selenide are used in infrared detectors and photocells due to their sensitivity to infrared radiation.


Medical imaging: Thallium-201 isotope are used in nuclear medicine for stress tests of the heart and detecting coronary artery disease through imaging.
