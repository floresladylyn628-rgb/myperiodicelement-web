Flourine is pale yellow, highly reactive, and toxic gaseous element, the most electronegative of all elements. It's the first element in the halogen group (group 17) on the periodic table. Flourine readily forms compounds with the most other elements, and its reactivity is due to its small atomic size and high electronegativity.

Flourine was first identified in 1771 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele, who isolated it in the form of hydroflouric acid. However, in the free element, flourine gas, was not isolated until 1886 by Henri Moissan, who used electrolysis to separate it. Moissan's work was significant and earned him the Nobel Prize in Chemistry.

Flourine has various applications, including:


Dental health: Flouride, a compound of flourine, is a key ingredient in toothpaste and mouthwash, helping prevent tooth decay by strengthening tooth enamel.


Nuclear Fuel Processing: Flourine is used in the production of uranium hexadlouride(UF6), a crucial component in the enrichment of uranium for nuclear fuel.


Platic and polymers: Flourine is a key component in the creation of fluoropolymers, such as Teflon(PTFE), known for its non-stick and heat resistance properties.
