Helium is a lightweight, colorless, odorless, tasteless, non-toxic, inert, monatomic gas. It's the second lightest and second most abundant element in the universe. Helium is a noble gas, which means it's unreactive and doesn't readily form compounds with other elements. Helium was discovered in the sun before it was found on earth. In 1998, French astronomer Pierre Janssen and British astronomer Norman Lockyer independently discovered helium while studying the sun's spectrum during a solar eclipse. They noticed a yellow line in the sun's spectrum that didn't match any known element.

In 1895, Scottish chemist William Ramsay and English chemist Lord Rayleigh isolated helium on Earth by treating the mineral cleveite with acid. The name "helium" comes from the Greek word "helios", meaning sun, reflecting its initial discovery in the sun's spectrum.

Helium has various applications, including:


Balloons and airships: Helium's low density makes it ideal for lifting objects.


Cryogenics: Liquid helium is used as a coolant in scientific research and applications.


Medical imaging: Helium is used in magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) machines.


Space exploration: Helium is used to pressurize rocket fuel tanks.
