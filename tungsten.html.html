Tungsten is a dense, silvery-gray metal known for having the highest melting point of all elements and exceptional hardness. Tungsten is resistant to wear, corrosion, and high temperatures, making it indispensible in demanding environments.

Tungsten was first identified in 1781 by Swedish chemist Carl Wilhelm Scheele, who discovered that a mineral called tungstenite contained a unique acidic compound, later named tungstic acid. However, the element itself was isolated two years later, in 1783, by Spanish chemists Juan Jose Elhuyar and Fausto Elhuyar, who successfully reduced tungsten oxide to produce the pure metal. They named it tungsten, derived from the Swedish words "tung sten", meaning "heavy stone", reflecting its density.

Tungsten has various applications, including:


Filaments: Used in incandescent light bulbs and vacuum tubes due to its ability to widthstand high temperatures without melting.


Medical imaging: Tungsten is used for X-ray targets and shielding materials in medical equipment.


Jewelry: Tungsten is used to make scratch-resistant rings and other jewelry due to its hardness and attractive appearance.
