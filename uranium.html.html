Uranium is a dense, silvery-gray, radioactive metal, belonging to the actinide series. It is best known for its role as a key fuel in nuclear reactors and weapons due to its fissile isotopes, particularly uranium-235 and uranium-238. Found naturally in the Earth's crust, uranium is weakly radioactive and occurs in minerals like pitchblende and uraninite.

Uranium was discovered in 1789 by German chemist Martin Heinrich Klaproth, who identified it whilr analyzing the mineral pitchblende. He named it uranium after the planet Uranus, which had been discovered just eight years earlier. Though its radioactive properties were unknown at the time, uranium gained significant attention in 1896 when Henri Becquerel discovered radioactivity while studying uranium salts. Later, marie and Pierre Curie expanded on this research, further uncovering the properties of radioactive elements.

Uranium has various applications, including:


Nuclear energy: Uranium-235 is used as a fuel in nuclear reactors for electricity generation due to its ability to undergo fission.


Radiation shielding: Uranium's density makes it useful for shielding against radiation in certain applications.


Military use: Depleted uranium is used in armor-piercing ammunition and tank armor for its high density and durability.
