Protactinium is a dense, silvery-gray, radiactive metal. It belongs to the actinide series and is relatively rare in nature, primarily found in uranium ores.

Protactinium was first identified in 1913 by Kasimir Fajans and Hoswald Helmuth Gohring, who discovered the isotope protactinium-234 and initially named it brevium due to its short half-life. Later, in 1918, Otto Hahn and Lise Meitner, along with Frederick Soddy and John Cranston, independently isolated the longer-lived isotope protactinium-231, leading to its renaming as protactinium, reflecting its position as the precursor to actinium in the decay chain.

Protactinium has various applications, including:


Material science: Occasionally used as a tracer in research due to its isotopic properties.


Nuclear fuel studies: It plays a role in examining uranium and thorium fuel cycles, as it is part of the decay series.


Scientific research: Protactinium is studied in nuclear and radiochemistry to better understand actinide behavior and radioactive decay chains.
