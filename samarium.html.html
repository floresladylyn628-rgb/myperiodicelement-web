Samarium is a silvery-white, moderately hard rare earth element, belonging to the lanthanide series. Samarium reacts slowly with water but quickly with acids, forming hydrogen gas and samarium salts.

Samarium was discovered in 1879 by French chemist Paul Emile Lecoq de Boisbaudran, who isolated it from the mineral samarskite , a rare earth ore found in the Ural Mountains. The element was named after "Vasily Samarsky-Bykhovets", a Russian mining engineer whi first identified samarskite, marking the only instance of an element named after a person without scientific contributions.

Samarium has various applications, including:


Nuclear reactors: Samarium-149 acts as a neutron absorber to regulate nuclear fission in reactors.


Optical devices: Used in infrared-absorbing glass, lasers, and infrared sensors.


Cancer treatment: Samarium-153 is used in radiotherapy to alleviate pain in bone cancer patients and as a medical tracer.
