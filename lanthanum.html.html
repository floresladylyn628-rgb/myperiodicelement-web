Lanthanum is a soft, silvery-white metal. It is the first element in the lanthanide series. It is highly reactive, especially in air, where it forms an oxide layer, and it reacts readily in water. Lanthanum is not found in its pure form in nature but occurs in minerals like monazite and bastnasite.

Lanthanum was discovered in 1839 by Swedish chemist Carl Gustaf Mosander. While working with a mineral cerite, Mosander noticed that it contained small amounts of an unknown substance. By carefully separating cerium from the mineral, he isolated the new element and named it lanthanum, derived from the Greek word "lanthanein," meaning "to lie hidden" or "to escape notice", reflecting its subtle presence in cerite.

Lanthanum has various applications, including:


Catalysts: Lanthanum oxide is used as a catalyst in cracking crude oil during petroleum refining to produce gasoline and other fuels.


Batteries: Lanthanum is a key component in nickel-metal hyydride batterries, which are commonly used in hybrid vehicles like the Toyota Prius.


Lighting: Lanthanum is used in carbon arc lamps, which were historically important for studio lighting and projectors.
