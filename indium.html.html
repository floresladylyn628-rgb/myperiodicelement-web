Indium is a chemical element, symbolized as In, and is a soft, malleable, silvery-white metal. It's used in various applications, including semiconductors, solders, flat-panel displays, and alloys, due to its unique properties. Indium is relatively rare and often extracted as a byproduct of zinc ore processing.

Indium was discovered in 1863 by German chemists Ferdinand Reich and Hieronymous Theodor Richter. They discovered it while analyzing sphalerite (zinc ore) using spectroscopy. The name "indium" comes from the indigo blue line in its atomic spectrum. Indium is a relatively rare element and is often extracted as a byproduct of zinc ore processing.

Indium has various applications, including:


Flat-panel displays: Indium tin oxide (ITO) is used as a transparent electrode in flat-panel displays, such as LCDs and touchscreens.


Soldering: Indium is used in solders, particularly for applications requiring low melting points and high reliability.


Solar cells: Indium is used in some types of solar cells, including copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS) solar cells.
