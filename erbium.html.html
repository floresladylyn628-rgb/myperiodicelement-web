Erbium is a silvery-white, soft, and malleable rare earth metal with atomic number 68, belonging to the lanthanide series.

Erbium was discovered in 1843 by Swedish chemist Carl Gustaf Mosander while analyzing the mineral gadolinite, found in the Ytterby mine near Stockholm, Sweden. Mosander separated gadolinite into several distinct rare earth elements, including erbium, terbium, and yttrium. Initially, erbium was isolated as an oxide, which Mosander named after Ytterby, the site of its discovery.

Erbium has various applications, including:


Fiber optics: Erbium is used as a dopant in optical fibers for telecommunications, enabling efficient signal amplification in erbium-doped fiber amplifiers.


Lasers: Erbium-doped lasers are utilized in medical procedures, such as dermatology and dentistry, due to their precision and ability to work in the infrared spectrum.


Glass and ceramics: Erbium compounds are used to impart a pink color to glasses and glazes, often for decorative purposes.
