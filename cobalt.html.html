Cobalt is a hard, ferromagnetic, with the atomic number 27 and symbol Co. It's known for its high melting point, corrosion resistance, and magnetic properties, making it useful in applications like batteries, magnets, and high-strength alloys. Cobalt is also essential for certain biological processes and has various industrial uses.

Cobalt was discovered by Swedish chemist Georg Brandt in 1735. The name "cobalt" comes from the German word "Kobold," meaning "goblin" or "evil spirit," likely due to the toxic nature of cobalt compounds and the troublesome nature of cobalt ore in the smelting process. Cobalt has since become a crucial element in various industrial applications, including batteries, magnets, and alloys.

Cobalt has various applications, including:


Batteries: Cobalt is used in lithium-ion batteries for electric vehicles and portable electronics.


Pigments: Cobalt compounds are used as pigments in glass, ceramics, and paints.


Magnets: Cobalt is used to make strong permanent magnets, particularly in Alnico magnets.
