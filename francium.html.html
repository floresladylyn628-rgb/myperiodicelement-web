Francium is highly radioactive alkali metal and is one of the rarest naturally occurring elements on Earth. It is produced during the decay of actinium and exists in trade amounts in uranium and thorium ores.

Francium was discovered in 1939 by Marguerite Perey, a French Physicist, while studying the decay of actinium. She identified it as the missing element 87 from the periodic table. Francium was named after France, Perey's home country, in recognition of her groundbreaking work.

Francium has various applications, including:


Nuclear physics: It helps researchers understand radioactive decay chains and interactions involving unstable isotopes.


Medical research: Though rarely practical, francium's properties are explored for potential radiological applications, such as cancer treatments.


Scientific research: Francium is used in atomic structure studies, particularly to investigate how electrons behave in heavy atoms.
