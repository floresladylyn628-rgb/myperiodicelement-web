Radium is a radiactive alkaline earth metal. It is formed naturally from the decay of uranium and thorium and emits intense alpha radiation.

Radium was discovered in 1898 by Marie and Pierre Curie while studying the mineral pitchblende(uranium ore). They isolated radium as a new element due to its intense radiactivity. The name radium comes from the Latin word "radius," meaning ray, reflecting its ability to emit rays of radiation.

Radium has various applications, including:


Scientific research: Radium played a key role in early studies of radioactivity, helping scientists understand nuclear decay and radiation.


Medical use: Radium was once used in radiation therapy to treat cancers, relying on its radioactive emissions to destroy malignant cells.


Luminous Paints: Radium was used in glow-in-the-dark paints for watch dials, instrument panels, and clocks, though this practice ended due to health risks.
