Terbium is a silvery-gray, soft, and ductile rare earth metal with atomic number 65, belonging to the lanthanide series. Terbium is known for its luminescent properties, particularly in green phosphors used in LEDs and displays.

Terbium was discovered in 1843 by Swedish chemist Carl Gustaf Mosander while analyzing the mineral gadolinite, which was found in the quarry near the village of Ytterby in Sweden. Mosander separated terbium as an impurity from yttrium oxide and named it after Ytterby, the same place that inspired the names of other elements like yttrium, erbium, and ytterbium. Initially, terbium was difficult to isolate in pure form due to the chemical similarities of the rare earth elements. It wasn't until advanced separation techniques were developed in the 20th century that terbium could be fully isolated and studied.

Terbium has various applications, including:


Energy-efficient lighting: Incorporated in flourescent light bulbs to improve efficiency and color rendering.


Lasers: Terbium compounds are used in solid-state lasers for specialized optical applications.


Fuel Cells: Terbium-stabilized materials are used in solid oxide fuel cells to improve durability and performance.
