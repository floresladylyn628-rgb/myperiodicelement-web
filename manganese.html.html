Manganese is a hard, silvery-white transition metal with the atomic number 25 and symbol Mn. It's known for its role in steel production, where it adds strength, hardness, and durability. Manganese is also used in batteries, catalysts, and various industrial applications.

Manganese was discovered by Carl Wilhelm Scheele in 1774, and first isolated by Johan Gottlieb Gahn in 1774. The name "manganese" comes from the Latin word "magnesia," referring to a type of manganese ore. Manganese has been used for centuries, with ancient civilizations using it to color glass and ceramics. Today, manganese is a crucial element in steel production and various industrial applications.

Manganese has various applications, including:


Steel production: Manganese is a key alloying element in steel, enhancing strength, hardness, and durability.


Welding rods: Manganese is used in welding rods to improve weld quality.


Batteries: Manganese dioxide is used in alkaline batteries.
