Beryllium is a steel-gray, hard, strong, lightweight and brittle alkaline earth metal. It is a divalent element that occurs naturally only in combination with other elements to form minerals. Beryllium is also known for its high strenght-to-weight ratio, high melting point, and ability to withstand high temperatures.

Beryllium is a rare element in the universe and is not formed during the Big Bang or in the cores Of stars. Instead, it is primarily produced in supernova explosions through a process called cosmic ray spallation. Beryllium was discovered(1798) as the oxide by French chemist Nicolas-Louis Vauquelin in beryl and in emeralds and was isolated (1828) as the metal independently by German chemist Friedrich Wohler and French chemist Antoine A.B Bussy by the reduction of its chloride with potassium.

Beryllium has various applications, including:


Aerospace and defense: Beryllium's high strenght-to-weight ratio makes it ideal for aircraft and spacecraft components, including parts for satellites and missiles.


Electronics: Beryllium is used in X-ray tubes as windows due to its transparency to X-rays.


Industrial applications: Lithium is used in the production of greases, ceramics, and other materials.
