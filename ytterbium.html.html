Ytterbium is a soft, silvery-white metal belonging to the lanthanide series of rare earth elements. Its relatively stable, filled 4f orbital makes it less chemically reactive compared to other lanthanides.

Ytterbium was discovered in 1878 by Swiss chemist Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac while analyzing the mineral gadolinite, which was sourced from the Ytterby mine in Sweden. Marignac identified a new rare earth element within gadolinite and named it ytterbia, after the village of Ytterby, which has also lent its name to several other elements.

Ytterbium has various applications, including:


Fiber Optic Communications: Ytterbium-doped optical fibers amplify signals in long-distance communication systems.


Atomic clocks: The stable spectroscopic transitions of ytterbium isotopes, like Yb-171, are used in highly precise atomic clocks.


Portable x-ray machines: The radioactive isotope Yb-169 emits gamma rays and is used as a compact radiation source in x-ray portable devices.
