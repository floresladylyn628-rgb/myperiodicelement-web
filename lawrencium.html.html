Lawrencium is a synthetic, radioactive actinide metal. As the last actinide, it exhibits properties properties similar to other actinides, but its short half-life and instability limit its use to scientific research in nuclear chemistry.

Lawrencium was first synthesized in 1961 at the Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory in California by a team led by Albert Ghiorso, Torbjorn Sikkeland, and Robert M. Latimer. It was named after Ernest O. Lawrence, the physicist who invented the cyclotron, a particle accelerator crucial for creating synthetic elements.

Lawrencium has no practical applications outside of scientific research due to its radioactivity, short half-life, and difficulty in production. It is primarily used in experimental studies to understand the chemical behavior of actinides and superheavy elements.
