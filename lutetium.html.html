Lutetium is a silvery-white, dense, and hard metal that marks the end of the lanthanide series. Lutetium is sometimes debated as the first element of the d-block rather than the last lanthanide.

Lutetium was discovered independently in 1907 by two chemists: Georges Urbain of France and Carl Auer von Welsbach of Austris. Both were working to separate rare earth elements from a sample of ytterbia. Urbain named the new element "lutecium", derived from "Lutecia," the ancient Roman name of Paris, the city where he conducted his research. The name was later standardized to "lutetium."

Lutetium has various applications, including:


Medical imaging: Lutetium oxyorthosilicate crystals are used in PET scanners for detecting gamma rays.


Cancer Therapy: Lutetium-177 isotope is used in targeted radiation treatments for tumors.


Geological dating: Lutetium-176 is used in radiometric dating to determine the age of rocks and meteorites.
