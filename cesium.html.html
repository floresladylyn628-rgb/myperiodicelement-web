Cesium is a soft, silvery-gold alkali metal with the atomic number 55. It is highly reactive and one of the most electropositive and least abundant naturally occurring element. Cesium reacts explosively with water and air, making it extremely reactive.

Cesium was discovered in 1860 by German chemists Robert Bunsen and Gustav Kirchhoff using a spectroscope, a tool they had recently developed to analyze a light spectra. It was the first element discovered using spectroscopic methods, and its name comes from the Latin word "caesius," meaning "sky blue", due to the bright blue lines in its emission spectrum.

Cesium has various applications, including:


Atomic clocks: Cesium -133 is used in atomic clocks, which are among the most accurate timekeeping devices in the world.


Oil and gas industry: Cesium formate, a dense and non-corrosive liquid, is used as a drilling fliud in oil and gas exploration.


Medical applications: Cesium isotopes, such as cesium-137, are used in radiotherapy for cancer treatment.
